Alan Turing

The Father of Theoretical Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence

Alan Turing was an English mathematician, computer scientist, and philosopher who famously conceptualized the Turing Machine and contributed to the cryptanalysis of Nazi ciphers in World War II.

Born on June 23, 1912, in Maida Vale, London, Alan Turing was the younger of the two sons of Julius Mathison Turing and Ethel Sara Turing.

During his time in school, he was very interested in the studies of mathematics and science, and it was at Sherborne School that he met Christopher Morcom, someone else who shared his love on the topics. After the death of Morcom, their relationship inspired Turing to put even more time and energy into his work.

Later, in university, Turing published papers about what we now know as Turing machines, and how they could perform any algorithm so long as they were computable.

As World War II broke out, Turing became a leading participant of breaking the German cipher, and in particular, the Enigma. He improved on the Polish machine bomba and his method of using statistical techniques to rule out certain settings of the Enigma machine has been said to have contributed to ending the war two years earlier, and saving more than 14 million lives.

Turing's work cannot be understated, and his research and ideas have lead to advancements such as the modern computer, and better approaches in the field of cryptanalysis. It is safe to say that without Turing, modern computing would likely not be the way it is today.
